Background                Higher Education        has unexpectedly catapulted itself straight into the digital         age ,         and as a result University's are now seriously looking at how they can centralise platforms to sort out the mess of student career development. At the very heart of this is the internship - a whole lot more than some optional experience, but         actually a                 really vital         step which is supposed to give students a real flavour of what working life is         actually like        , and the connections that should last them a lifetime. But it's a bit of a disaster out there - students at Uni's around the world are struggling to find decent internships because the fact is they just don't have the right connections and there is a complete shambles of how things get sorted out (        Golhar         et al., 2024)                 Things haven't moved forward very much for UCSI University, the whole internship process is still a bit of a clunky affair with lecturers and students doing a bit of fumbling to make it all work. And in all honesty, this is just a symptom of the really big problem - internship listings are literally all over the place - job websites, company websites and getting lost in a mess of career centers - so students are left scrambling to find a job which actually fits the skills they've been learning at Uni - it's a total nightmare (Phyo &amp; Khine, 2025;         Mydyti         &amp; Kadriu, 2020)                  But,         research has made it clear - there's got to be a better way - an Internship Management System (IMS) that can bring everyone together and get things a lot more streamlined. And that's exactly what UniJobLink is all         about ,         its a one-stop-shop where students can create a profile and apply for internships which         actually match         what they've been studying - and for companies, a quick and easy way to post job openings &amp; review job candidates with minimal hassle. Now if UCSI were to take a leaf out of UniJobLink's book and get a centralized platform in place that's all tied in with the University - it could really help bridge the gaps and make the whole internship process a whole lot easier for everyone - students, lecturers &amp; companies alike (Phyo &amp; Khine, 2025;         Mydyti         &amp; Kadriu, 2020)                       Problem Statement             The internship process at UCSI University is running into some pretty big problems that affect students, lecturers and industry partners in roughly the same way. For starters, UCSI students have a tough time finding the right internships because there's no one place to look - literally no real central hub for listings. Meanwhile lecturers are stuck manually chasing up students, trying to keep tabs on their progress. And industry partners - well they're having a hard time finding students they want to work with because all the opportunities seem to be hiding all over the place. This creates a situation where loads of talent      is      being wasted and loads of opportunities are being missed by both sides. It’s a particular problem within the internship process at UCSI especially how students and their supervisors talk to      each     . It all leads to a tangled web of delays and inefficiencies. Without a smart system in place the university can't really compete in the wider job market - it’s far too slow and prone to mistakes. (     Golhar      et al., 2024;      Mydyti      &amp; Kadriu, 2020)           The idea behind this project is to get a      centralised      internship system up and running that makes life easier for everyone who's involved. As things stand, UCSI students and their supervisors are still stuck with a manual process that lets the university down. This gap between what we've got now and what we want in terms of a super-smart digital system is why things keep falling through the cracks - we can’t even keep track of who's applying for what. We’re going to use a tried and tested approach, called System Development Life      Cycle      to build a simple web-based system that ties together three key things: submissions, internships and automated updates for supervisors. By doing this, we should be able to streamline the whole internship process making it easier for students to get a foot in the door and making life much easier for lecturers as well. Studies have shown this is the way forward too. (     Golhar      et al., 2024). Proposed Solution                     Develop a unified, Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) web application using the MERN stack. This platform will serve as a single digital hub where Companies can instantly post verified opportunities and Students can utilize a Category Filter Engine to find roles that strictly match their degree.                     Integrate a Real-Time WebSocket Direct Messaging Engine directly into the platform. To maintain professional boundaries and prevent spam, this chat interface will execute "Acceptance Logic," strictly unlocking ONLY after an HR recruiter formally accepts a student's ATS application.                    Implement a dedicated Lecturer/Admin Module. This module features a rigid SSM-Verification gateway to ensure only legitimate companies can interact with UCSI students, alongside a Global Faculty Dashboard that provides automated, real-time analytics tracking exactly which students have secured placements. Scope                    The scope of this project encompasses the development of a fully integrated, role-based, multi-tenant web application strictly utilizing the MERN software stack (React.js, Node.js, Express, alongside a MySQL relational database). The system acts as a centralized university hub seamlessly isolating operations across three core modules:                     Student Module        :            Verified UCSI undergrads get to search for live internships on the system. A pretty useful feature here is the      category filter      - it      lets      students sort opportunities by industry - say IT or Healthcare - or location. They can even upload their resume as a PDF and keep an eye on how far their applications have got       .                     Company (HR) Module        :            This is an Applicant Tracking System (ATS) for registered corporations to put up job openings and deal with candidate applications as they come in. The system runs a pretty strict process for the HR people - they can filter by role, look at academic records and make a yes or no decision (Candidate in or out)       .                     Lecturer / Admin Module        :            the lecturer / admin section is all about keeping things secure for the lecturers. We set things up so that the admin      has to      okay any new company signing up with an SSM number before they're allowed to start interacting with the students       .                        Communication Subsystem (In-App Messaging)        :        A Real-Time WebSocket Chat Engine embedded directly into the platform. To strictly preserve professional flow, this interface is exclusively accessible         only after        a company has formally accepted a student's resume in the ATS pipeline, allowing secure interview scheduling without external email dependencies. Database Entity-Relationship (ER) Diagram            ERD                                                                          Figure         2        :            Database Entity-Relationship (ER) Diagram                          Key Relationships &amp; Cardinality Explained:                   The 1-to-1 RBAC Identity Layer:            basically, the USER table is the main collection point for all authentications. It          got      a primary key 'id' which links directly to the three other tables - STUDENT, COMPANY, and ADMIN - which all have a foreign key called 'user_id' pointing back to the USER table. Because of this link, one person who's logged in can only be one      particular student     , company, or admin profile.                   The 1-to-Many Job Lifecycle:            one company can post loads of different job openings and the JOB_POSTING table links each one back to its company using 'company_id'. There's also an APPLICATION table - this links a student applying for a specific job by 'student_id' to that job by 'job_id'. This means that if a company were to delete a job posting, any apps submitted for that job would be handled neatly without leaving any leftovers in the database       .                   The Many-to-1 Messaging Logic:             In order to      keep messaging between students and companies secure, we link a company up with a student in a specific room in the CHAT_ROOM table, called company_id and student_id. Then the MESSAGE table just collects messages in that room, with each message tied to that specific room by chat_room_     id, and      also showing who the message came from via sender_user_id       . User Experience (UX) Principles                 Looking at these UI wireframes, you get a good idea of the system's layout, but how does it         actually feel         to use it? To create a seamless and top-notch experience, we've built our system around these core UX design principles that really make a difference       :                     Frictionless Application Flow (The 3-Click Rule)        :            The 3-Click Rule - basically, a student should be able to log in, search for an internship, and      submit an application      all in three clicks or under. No messing around, no getting frustrated and giving up.                        Intelligent Role Redirection        :               Our onboarding process is fully automated - and it's         actually pretty         clever. As soon as a user logs in, the system quickly works out their role and whisks them off to the right dashboard. So, a company HR rep isn't suddenly staring at a screen full of menus for students.                        Real-Time Feedback Loops        :               Good UX is all about keeping users calm and confident. We've built in real-time feedback loops, so users get instant visual confirmation - like a nice little green toast pop-up when they upload their resume, or a red notification badge when an interview is booked. It's reassuring to know the system has their back.                        Error Prevention &amp; Graceful Handling        :               We've made sure input forms (like job postings) do instant validation on the fly. So, if a user forgets to fill in a required field, the UX will quietly tell them what they're missing and how to fix it - no need to go through the whole submission process again and get that dreaded server response error. High-Level System Architecture Diagram                                                                            Figure         2        4        :         High-Level System Architecture Diagram                   This        diagram shows you the journey that data takes from the time you click a button or submit a form. First off, you interact with the User Interface on the React Frontend - that's the bit you see on your screen, the presentation layer. When you click a button or fill out a form, Axios takes that action and wraps it up into a neat little HTTP request (this is what's usually sent from a website to a server). It then sends that request to the Node/Express Server - that's the part that does all the heavy lifting, running the application layer. Once the request has arrived, the server springs into action - it checks to see if the security token that your user account is sending along is valid. If all checks out, the business controllers take over and start running through the logic, then head down to the MySQL Database (that's where all the data lives, the data layer). They store or retrieve what you need and then send the answers all the way back up - back to the server, and then the server sends the results all back up the chain to you. Technical Infrastructure Architecture:                      The technical infrastructure driving the application validates the assumptions established in this chapter. The system acts as a modernized         Client-Server RESTful API Architecture        decoupling the React Frontend from a scalable Express/MySQL Backend.                   Frontend Web Architecture:            We're using React.js because of its ability to make dynamic navigation a breeze with custom component routing. That means users get instant access to the pages they want without having to wait around for a whole page to reload. For our responsive web design (RWD) we're using either Tailwind CSS or Bootstrap 5 to make sure our grids scale down nicely for mobile users and don't look clunky.                   Backend Application Logic:            This time around we're running Express.js (Node.js) which makes it super easy to take incoming HTTP requests and map them directly to JSON payloads, rather than rendering the whole page every time. As for document      storage      we're using Multer to stream in PDF      files      so they get saved safely into a locked away local /uploads/ directory.                      Database &amp; Integrity:               When it comes to storing         data         we're using MySQL Server to make sure our table schemas are sticklers for the rules - so if someone tries to delete a user and then tries to orphan a related job application, the system stops them dead in their tracks. Then we're using         Sequelize         to scrub our data payloads clean to prevent SQL injection attacks from getting a foothold.                    Security Protocols:            When it comes to managing      sessions      we're using JSON Web Tokens (JWT) instead of those awkward server cookies to keep things nice and secure. Passwords get hashed and salted with Bcrypt.js before they even get stored in the database, and we've got Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) running in the background to make sure cross-user route access is blocked off at the middleware level.
